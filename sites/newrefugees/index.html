  • Being an Ex-pat vs. Being a Refugee

    RADICALLY RESPONSIBLE CHOOSING

    The difference between being an Ex-pat and being a Refugee is which Story you Choose to empower, and whether your choice of Story is Conscious or Unconscious.

     

    Choosing is one of your 3 Powers as a human being. The other two powers are Declaring and Asking. You are already using all three powers in your daily life, but until you make these powers conscious, you use them unconsciously to create a life that can only serve Unconscious Purposes.

     

    It is possible to take Radical Responsibility for Choosing to be where you are.


    Most of us thoughtlessly use the Standard Human Intelligence Thoughtware (S.H.I.T.) that modern culture gives us from our families and from school. In Standard Human Intelligence Thoughtware, choosing is unconscious. The meme of making choices conscious is not offered.

     

    An Ex-pat unconsciously chooses to live in the story-world that they are where they are - which is presumably in a country or culture that is different from their birth culture - by their own choice... for the reason that gives them a better life.

     

    A Refugee, on the other hand, unconsciously chooses to live in the story-world that they are forced to be where they are by the circumstances... and they are there for the reason that if they did not come here they would not survive.

     

    Yes, these two stories are different - that use by the Ex-pat and that used by the Refugee, but In both cases the choice of which story they use has been made unconsciously.


    It is exciting to discover the Possibility of consciously making the choice about being Ex-pat or a Refugee, regardless of the circumstances. Such a choice does not depend on the reason of the circumstances because the choice is made according to your own Authority.

     

    Taking back your Authority to Choose means that no one can make your choice for you. More interestingly, no one can stop you from making this choice yourself. You can reclaim your Authority to make this choice about which Story you use.


    When choices start to become Conscious, there is an additional Possibility that you can claim for yourself.

     

    This is new Possibility is the option of choosing the outcome that is there, even if it seems that no other option was available for you to choose from. This is called 'choosing what is'.

     

    How can you call it 'making a choice' if there is only one option to choose from?

     

    Excellent question!

     

    The choice you get to make is actually the choice between being a Low Drama Victim of the circumstances, or being in High Drama Radically Responsible Creator of those exact same circumstances in which you have but one option to choose from and have chosen it.

     

    By choosing what is, you are no longer a Victim of what is, but have become the Creator of what is, because now you have consciously chosen it. Your choosing can be real even if it is beyond other people's permissible comprehension because to them is seems illogical or unreasonable.


    By standing in your natural Adulthood Authority and making the conscious Radically Responsible choice to choose what is, for example, "Yes. I choose to be here where I am," then no matter how you physically came to be where you are, you have killed the Refugee's Victim Story and transformed yourself from being a Refugee into being an Ex-pat.


    This is a powerful option open to actually anyone.

  • What Is A New Refugee?

    In June 2019 the United Nations Refugee Agency (UNHCR) released a report documenting that over 70 million people around the world are now refugees. The UN agency admitted that their estimate is conservative (meaning underestimated).

     

    UNHCR defines a 'refugee' in three limited categories: refugees, asylum seekers, and internally displaced people (IDPs), all of whom must be fleeing conflict or persecution to qualify as a 'refugee'.

     

    The UN's figures ignore the estimated 10 million stateless persons. Neither do they include the increasing numbers of people who are forced to leave their homes or countries due to climate change. Typhoons, draught, hurricanes, tornadoes, rising temperatures, and rising oceans may add an additional 150 million actual refugees by 2050, forcing the world total of refugees to gradually exceed 200 million.

     

    This total - as shocking as they may be - are joined by a third category of refugees that is not taken into account by any of the above calculations.

     

    There are estimated to be more than 100 million Cultural Creatives already in the world today.

    • Cultural Creatives are refugees because they are forced to abandon a commonly held belief system due to their increased awareness that the old belief system is crippled and blocking evolution. 
    • Cultural Creatives are refugees because they take responsibility for living, working, and playing in gameworlds of their own making which support values of their own liking.

    However, a refugee is a refugee, regardless of the way you have become a refugee.

     

    To think that you becoming a refugee is an accident does not pay proper respects to the brilliant wisdom and timing of ECCO (Earth Coincidence Control Office) which has been arranging the miraculous events of your life heretofore.

     

    If becoming a refugee is not an accident, then what do you need to shift to make effective use of the exquisite and powerful opportunities you have been given by becoming a refugee? That is what this website is about: tools, techniques, thoughtware upgrades, and processes that activate you as a transformational refugee.

     

    The perspective that being a refugee carries a higher state of Possibility than, for example, being an employee, being a citizen of a country, owning a home or a business, etc., is not a new idea. For example, at the end of the article at this link you will find these words: "Instead of being framed using linguistic strategies that suggest victimhood... refugees and asylum seekers could perhaps better be framed as capable, resilient people who have overcome adversity, who have resisted and freed themselves from oppressive or dangerous situations."

    NEW REFUGEE POSSIBILITY TEAM MEETINGS

    Refugee Possibility Team Pilot Design (RPTPD v1.0)

    Purpose:

    To activate nonmaterial value creation, emotional healing, resilience, and regenerative social architectures among displaced persons by introducing Possibility Team practices into refugee camps, informal settlements, and host‑community peripheries.

    Core Principles:

    Everyone in a refugee context is already a practitioner of survival.

    Possibility Teams shift survival into conscious evolution.

    Refugees already live in liminal space. Possibility Teams give them tools to navigate it responsibly.

    Nonmaterial value becomes a portable, sovereign, and unstealable currency.

    Pilot Components:

    1. Scouting & Invitation

    Collaborate with local NGOs or grassroots aid organizations already embedded in camps or settlements.

    Frame Possibility Teams as “shared circles of practice and resilience,” not therapy, not religion, not politics.

    Identify 1–2 “Edge Initiators” — residents willing to step into a spaceholder role (can be trained in situ).

    2. Initial Ritual Activation

    Hold an opening ritual in a safe public or semi‑private space — symbolically marking the Possibility Team’s inception.

    Include:

    • Each participant naming themselves aloud.
    • Lighting a candle as a symbol of sovereignty.
    • Sharing one thing each person wishes to create or resolve.

    3. Seed Practices

    Start with three foundational practices:

    Feelings Check‑In: Teach and model the 4 feelings and distinguish them from emotions.

    Box & Gremlin Mapping: Invite each participant to draw and name patterns of their Box & Gremlin.

    Possibility Questions: Open rounds of “What else is possible here that we haven’t seen yet?”

    These three practices alone can run 2–3 sessions and are enough to ignite the field.

    4. Frequency & Duration

    Weekly or bi‑weekly meetings of 60–90 minutes.

    Each meeting starts with a ritual (lighting the candle, names) and ends with each person declaring an experiment they’ll try before the next meeting.

    5. Scaling & Multiplying

    Encourage participants to invite others after 3–4 sessions.

    Train emerging spaceholders to split and multiply circles as they grow (like mycelium).

    Role of Archans / Possibilitators:

    Remote mentoring: use video calls to support local Edge Initiators.

    Supply nonmaterial resources: laminated distinction maps, translated EHP scripts, story cards.

    Create feedback loops: keep channels open to hear their innovations and challenges.

    Pilot Locations to Consider:

    Lesbos, Greece (Moria replacement camps)

    Sudan–Chad border settlements

    Venezuelan refugees in Colombia

    Rohingya camps in Bangladesh

    Syrian refugees in northern Jordan & Turkey

    REFUGEE POSSIBILITY TEAM MEETING AND EHPs

    Refugee Possibility Team: Ritual & EHPs

    Inaugural Ritual Script: Refugee Possibility Team

    Purpose: To anchor sovereignty, possibility, and connection in a context of displacement.

    Space Preparation

    Choose a quiet corner, shaded if outdoors.

    Prepare: one small candle (or even a match or small stone if no candle), and a circle of chairs or mats.

    Draw a rough circle on the ground with chalk, rope, or even a stick in the dirt.

    Script

    Spaceholder speaks:

    “This is a circle of possibility. Even though we live in a place of no‑place, we can create a space where each person matters, where feelings have names, and where the future is not yet written.”

    Everyone enters the circle one at a time, saying: “I am here.”

    Spaceholder lights the candle (or places the stone at the center):

    “This light is what we bring. We cannot be made smaller. What we give here cannot be stolen.”

    Each person speaks one thing they wish to create, heal, or learn. No discussion — just witnessing.

    Spaceholder closes:

    “We agree to meet again, to practice, to notice, to create. Even here, we remain human.”

    3 Adapted Emotional Healing Processes (EHPs)

    EHP 1: Naming the Lost

    For Grief and Sadness

    Invite each person to say the names of people, places, or parts of themselves they feel they have lost since leaving home.

    After each name, group responds: “We hear you. They are remembered.”

    Then: “What is still alive in you that they left behind?”

    EHP 2: The Box of Survival

    For Anger and Fear

    Give each participant a small paper and pencil.

    Ask: “What rules did you follow to survive? Which rules do not serve you anymore?”

    Have them tear up the paper into small pieces, naming the Box behaviors aloud as they tear.

    Group says: “You are free to choose differently.”

    EHP 3: Fear Into Action

    For Frozen Fear

    Each participant names a fear they carry.

    Group repeats it back: “You are afraid of ___.”

    Then ask: “If you had no choice but to act anyway, what small step could you take right now?”

    Group says: “We will stand with you while you take it.”

    These scripts can be printed or laminated in translation and delivered alongside the pilot design.
