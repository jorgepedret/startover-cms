  • What Is An Assumption?

    An "assumptions" is a memetic construct that is not required to be connected to reality.

    Your Being can assume anything about anything.

    It takes self observation and introspection to discern whether what you are using to think with (your thoughtware) is an Assumption or not.

    Ignoring this distinction is like not checking to see if the book you are reading is fiction or nonfiction.

     

    An Assumptions does not require you to look around inside of yourself at your internal reality

    (for example, at your level of unconscious fear that is being covered up by the Assumption),

    or to look around outside of yourself at external reality

    (for example, at how the current level of methane in the atmosphere (1888 ppb in June 2020) equates to a global temperature of +12 degrees Centigrade higher than what has been average over the past 800,000 years).

    In what circumstances did you make your Assumption?

    Why do you keep yourself restrained by this Assumption?

    What if you are finished with your Assumptions in the same way that a butterfly is finished with its cocoon as it flies into the world as an adult?

    How can you do that?

    ASSUMPTIONS ABOUT THE WORLD AND PEOPLE AROUND YOU

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.01

    You make assumptions about the world and the people around you all the time. Most assumptions you are not even aware of.

    For one week, write down in your BEEP!Book every assumption you are aware of.

    Write as a heading about these assumptions: My assumptions about the world and the people around me.

    Don't think about whether or not the assumption is correct in the sense of proven. For example, you might think that the assumption, "I assume that the sun will rise again tomorrow." is proven and logical.

    Write it in your BEEP!Book anyway.

    You may find that assumptions suddenly pop up from your subconscious that you didn't know you had. I myself have an assumption about this that I suddenly see things I'm dealing with all over the place.

    Write down all assumptions about the world and people around you in your BEEP!Book.

    ASSUMPTIONS ABOUT YOURSELF

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.02

    In this experiment, if you start writing down all the assumptions about yourself in your BEEP!Book, you might discover that your assumptions about yourself vary depending on the people you are dealing with or the situations you are in.

    This means that your assumptions are not always valid, but only from time to time.

    So in this experiment, briefly write a few bullet points about the surrounding circumstances each time to explore this.

    PROVEN ASSUMPTIONS

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.03

    It could be that your assumptions stick so stubbornly to you because you are convinced that they are necessarily correct. Because they are proven.
    In this experiment, you take all the assumptions about yourself (see Experiment 2).

    You write on a blank page in your BEEP!Book as a heading: Proven Assumptions.

    Then divide your page into 2 columns. In the left column, write the first assumption you wrote down in Experiment 2; an assumption about yourself. Next to it, in the right column, you write everything that proves that your assumption is true.

    You decide what is valid as evidence and what is not.
    Do this with all assumptions from experiment 2.

    ASSUMPTIONS FORM A VACUUM FOR EXPECTATIONS

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.04

    Expectations arise from assumptions.

    You can explore whether this is true in this experiment.

    Write down 20 expectations that you have. It is unimportant in which context these expectations stand.

    Write down even seemingly small expectations, such as: "I expect my neighbor to greet me back when I say hello," or "I expect my boss to pay my salary on time."
    Write them in your BEEP!Book one below the other on a blank left-hand page. (If the left-hand page is not enough, do not continue writing on the right-hand page, but on the next left-hand page).
    Then start writing the matching assumption on the right book page next to it.

    For example, in the case of the neighbor thing, my assumption would be, "I assume that my neighbor is also interested in a relaxed, friendly neighbor relationship."

    Go through the whole list like this.

    UNPROVEN ASSUMPTIONS

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.06

    You do not have an assumption of everything and everyone. There are probably many areas in your life for which you do not have an assumption.

    This experiment is about consciously creating assumptions and checking them in a 2nd step. I'll give you an example:
    I have no assumption about being able to communicate with an earthworm.

    Now, for this experiment, I change my mind and make an assumption: "I assume it is possible to communicate with an earthworm." This is an unproven assumption.

    In the next step of the experiment, I look for evidence to support my assumption.

    There may be an opportunity to talk to an animal scientist about the assumption.

    Are there books/movies about this topic?

    And of course I can just try it: get an earthworm from the garden, put it on my hand (or in a jar) and get in contact with it.
    Document your results and the evidence that your assumption is right or wrong.

    After a time that seems appropriate to you as a researcher, conclude the experiment.

    For example, you can write: I found 4 pieces of evidence that the assumption that you can communicate with an earthworm is correct.

    CHECK YOUR THOUGHTWARE

    Matrix Code ASSUMPTI.07

    To do this experiment, you must have done Experiment 1 and/or 2 before, because you need a set of assumptions to put under the microscope.
    Take the first assumption and write it down again. This assumption is a written down thought from your head.

    What you think with is called thoughtware. It is similar to the program in a computer.

    By changing the program, you get different results.

    Break the assumption sentence into its parts and put them one by one under your attention magnifying glass.

    Where do these parts come from?

    What opinions, interpretations, promises, beliefs, attitudes, and declarations are in each component? What do you feel when you explore this?
    For example, one of my assumptions is, "I assume there are happy marriages."
    I put the word "marriage" under the microscope as a meme.

    Why do I use the word? Where did I first hear it?

    What emotions rise up in me? And most importantly, what assumptions are in turn inherent in the word "marriage"? In my case, the assumption that an administration can certify that two people will stay together for life.
    You can also do this experiment together with a spaceholder. It might be helpful to have someone navigate the depths with you.

    Emotions may also surface that can lead to Emotional Healing Processes.
