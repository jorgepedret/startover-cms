      Meadowsweet / Mädesüß

      (COOKBOOK.69 for StartOver.xyz​)

      Filipendula ulmaria, commonly known as Meadowsweet or Mead Wort in English, and as Mädesüß in German, is a perennial herb in the family Rosaceae (the family of apples and roses), and grows in damp meadows. It is native throughout most of Europe and Western Asia (Near east and Middle east). It has been introduced and naturalized in North America. The botanical name 'ulmaria' means 'elmlike', in reference to its individual leaves which resemble those of the elm tree (Ulmus). Like slippery elm bark bark, Meadowsweet contains salicylic acid, which has long been used as a painkiller. In 1897, Felix Hoffmann created a synthetically altered version of salicin, derived from Meadowsweet, which caused less digestive upset than pure salicylic acid. The new drug, acetylsalicylic acid, was named 'aspirin' by Hoffmann's employer Bayer AG after the old botanical name for meadowsweet, Spiraea ulmaria.

      In short, by eating 1 or 2 fresh raw Meadowsweet / Mädesüß leaves, your headache will go away.

      A tea made from Filipendula ulmaria flowers or leaves has been used in traditional Austrian herbal medicine for the treatment of rheumatism, gout, infections, and fever.

      If you find Meadowsweet / Mädesüß growing wild you can transplant some to your herb garden, or you can order it from your local nursery.

      Catch 'Em Alive Trap

      (COOKBOOK.72 for StartOver.xyz​)

      From time to time it can happen that a furry little mouse becomes bothersome. Modern culture offers you the solution of using a killer mouse trap, or some kind of rodent poisoning, neither of which is an elegant Archan solution. In their place we offer you the home-made Archan Regenerative Culture Catch 'Em Alive Trap. A full-size PDF of the above drawing is available for free to download HERE. You build this device yourelf (with your kids...) out of a large empty recycled steel coffee can with both ends cut off using a can-opener. By hammering a nail into a piece of wood through the rear rim of the can near its edge in several places, you can wire quarter-inch (or 1 centimeter) square galvanized steel mesh to the back of the can, cut to the right size using steel snipping shears. Drill 4 holes through the wooden base of a standard wooden mouse trap on side where the metal loop goes when it is closed. Make the same 4 holes in the side of the can near the entrance, making sure that the axel of the spring is exactly at the edge of the can. Bolt the wooden mouse trap to the side of the can on the inside of the can. Then wire the trap door to the moving loop of wire on the mousetrap, so that when the loop tries to snap shut it holds the wire mesh door closed. To set the trap, apply peanut butter or cheese to the bait hook, and then set the trap bar as usual.
