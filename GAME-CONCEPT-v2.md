# A Game as Big as the World
## What it could be, what it feels like, how you get in

---

## The Four Inspirations

**The OA (Season 2)** — A powerful figure creates a game that shatters the membrane of ordinary reality. Playing it requires you to do things you couldn't have imagined doing before you entered. The game reframes everything. You can't go back to seeing the way you saw before.

**Ready Player One** — Massive scale. Real teams. Things that actually matter at stake. People playing together across distances, cultures, generations — not against each other, but toward something. The sense that what you do today is part of something much larger than what you can see from where you stand.

**Daemon** — A distributed system released into the world, invisible but real, that coordinates thousands of people toward a goal no single one of them can perceive in full. Each player sees their piece clearly. The whole only becomes visible from the pattern.

**The War Against the Chtorr — The Mode** — In David Gerrold's book series, humanity faces an existential threat unlike anything it has encountered. The response requires people to operate differently — not smarter, not better trained, but fundamentally changed in how they perceive, how they relate, how they act. "The Mode" is a training that strips away pretense, defenses, and the comfortable fog most people live in. People who have been through it are different. They can't be bullshitted — including by themselves. They see faster, bond faster, decide faster. They recognize each other immediately. And what they recognize is: *this person is actually here*. The Mode isn't inspiration — it's the closest existing analog to what this game produces in people who play it for real.

## What the Experience Is Like

Imagine waking up one morning and the world looks slightly different.

Not because anything changed externally. But because you've been in a conversation — with a small group of people, doing experiments, trying things, being honest — and something in how you see has shifted. The conversations you used to tolerate feel hollow in a new way. The things you used to avoid feel more available. You're not more spiritual or more evolved. You're just — more *here*.

That's the beginning.

Further in, it starts to feel less like personal growth and more like being part of something. You have a team — people you meet with regularly, in person, doing specific things together. Not a support group. Not a mastermind. Something harder to name: a small unit of people who have agreed to be honest with each other and to actually build something. You're accountable to them in a way you've never been accountable to anyone, because you chose it freely, and they're doing the same.

You start noticing that your team is one of many. There are other groups, other places, other people playing the same game with the same maps. When you meet them, something recognizable passes between you immediately. Not a secret handshake — more like recognizing someone who has been through a similar fire. The world, which was previously a fog of strangers, becomes populated with people you can actually find.

The missions are real. You don't fight virtual enemies. You do something in the actual world — something that takes something from you, that teaches you something, that changes something. It might be a conversation you've been avoiding for three years. It might be building something with your hands in a piece of land that needs tending. It might be facilitating a process for your neighborhood, or sitting with someone in genuine difficulty without trying to fix them, or creating something new that didn't exist before.

You start carrying a different question. Not "how do I succeed in this culture?" but "what does this culture actually need from me?" Not "how do I protect myself?" but "what am I willing to risk?" Not "what do I want?" but "what am I here to build?"

The game doesn't make you enlightened. It doesn't solve your problems. It doesn't take you out of the world. It puts you more fully into it — with better tools, with a team, with a sense that what you do matters beyond your own biography.

And beneath all of it, running like a low-frequency hum: the knowledge that you are helping build something. Not metaphorically. Actually. The world the game is trying to create is being built, piece by piece, in real places, by real people, including you.

## What the Game Could Be — Three Possibilities

### Possibility 1: The Living Experiment Network

The simplest version. No new technology. No platform.

A network of small groups, each running real-world experiments drawn from a shared framework. Each group meets regularly — in person, in the same physical space — to report on what they tried, what happened, what they learned. They use a common set of maps and tools to make sense of their experience.

Groups are connected to other groups. There is a shared log — not a social media feed but a record of experiments and what they produced. Over time, the network becomes a body of knowledge built from lived experience, not theory.

Entry is through finding an existing group or forming a new one. The experiments are the doorway. You don't have to believe anything. You just have to be willing to try something and report back honestly.

This version of the game could exist tomorrow. It requires almost nothing — just people willing to try things and talk about what happened.

**What makes it a game**: You're tracking something. There's a sense of progression — not as personal achievement but as growing capacity. The experiments get harder as your capacity grows. You're building something that didn't exist before, and you can see it accumulating.

---

### Possibility 2: The World With a Map

A layer of technology added to the first version: a living map that shows where groups are active, what they're working on, and what they're building.

The map makes visible what is normally invisible: the density of people doing serious work in a given region, the kinds of experiments happening, the regeneration projects underway. For the first time, people doing this work can see themselves as part of something geographically real.

Groups use the map to find each other. Individuals looking for a team use it to find a group near them. Teams with overlapping purposes can see each other and begin collaborating.

The map also tracks aggregate progress toward something — a collective indicator of how much real change is accumulating across the network. Not a gamified score, but a reading: *this is where we are, this is what it will take, this is what we're building toward together.*

**The addition over Possibility 1**: Scale and visibility. The game becomes legible at a level where individual players can feel the larger movement they're part of. The sense of "I am not alone in this" becomes visual, geographic, real.

---

### Possibility 3: The Full Game — Teams, Missions, Thresholds, Network

The game at full expression.

Teams of 3 to 13 people form around a shared purpose and a piece of territory — a neighborhood, a watershed, a community, an institution — that they take responsibility for. The team has a declared purpose. It has a culture — specific practices, agreements, ways of meeting, ways of making decisions. It builds something over time in its territory.

Missions are issued — not by a central authority, but by the intersection of where the team is, what the team is ready for, and what the whole network needs. Some missions are individual. Some are team missions. Some require coordination with other teams in ways that are only possible if everyone is tracking the same map.

As people go through genuine change — not checked boxes, but real threshold crossings — they gain access to new kinds of missions, new layers of the network, new responsibilities. Not status. Responsibility.

There is a layer of coordination that runs across the whole network — something like a distributed intelligence, assembled from people who have been playing long enough and honestly enough to perceive the patterns of the whole. They don't make decisions for anyone. They make the shape of the whole visible to those who are ready to see it.

The game tracks toward a specific tipping point: the point at which enough people are building enough regenerative culture that a phase transition becomes visible — in the morphogenetic field, in actual communities, in the quality of what humans are making together. Not utopia. Just the beginning of something different.

**This version requires**: A platform, a coordination layer, a trained network of people who can witness threshold crossings and assign missions. It takes years to build. It requires the first two possibilities to work first.

## The Doorways — How People Get In

There is no single door. There are several, and they are all real.

**Doorway 1: An Experiment**
You read something. You find a specific experiment. It's concrete — something you can actually do this week. You do it. Something changes, even slightly. You want to know what's next. You go looking. This is how most people actually find their way in.

The game's single most important technology is a library of real experiments — not exercises, not journaling prompts, not meditations, but actual things to do in the world that produce real, reportable results.

**Doorway 2: A Person**
Someone you respect is different in a way you can't quite name. More present. Less defended. More willing to say difficult things clearly and without cruelty. You ask what they're doing. They tell you about the game. You don't believe it will work for you. You try an experiment anyway.

This doorway scales through personal credibility. The most powerful recruitment mechanism is people who have actually changed.

**Doorway 3: A Team**
You find a group of people who are doing something real — meeting regularly, trying things, being honest about what's working and what isn't. The group isn't performing transformation. It's actually attempting it. You want to be in a room with people like that. You join.

The formation of new teams is one of the game's most important engines. It should be as easy as possible to start a team and as hard as possible to fake your way through being one.

**Doorway 4: A Crisis**
Your life is changing in ways you didn't choose. Something is breaking down. The person you thought you were is not working anymore. The strategies that got you here won't get you further. You are, as the framework would say, at the edge.

This is not the worst entry point. It may be the best. The game is designed for people who are genuinely ready — not because they scheduled readiness, but because reality delivered it. Crisis is a delivery mechanism for readiness, and the game should be ready when reality delivers.

**Doorway 5: The Map**
You find the map and you see yourself on it — or rather, you see where you would be on it, and you see that there are others already there. The map makes the game visible from outside before you enter it. Some people will find their way in through the evidence of what the game is already producing.

## Use Cases

**A person in their late 30s who built a successful career and can't figure out why it feels hollow**
They've done therapy. They've read the books. They've attended the workshops. They still feel like they're playing someone else's game. They find the experiment library. They try one specific experiment about what they're actually feeling, not what they're performing. Something cracks open slightly. They find a team. Over two years they redesign their life not by quitting their career but by building a second context — a team, a purpose, a specific piece of work in the world that is actually theirs. The career either transforms or they leave it, but either way they're no longer hollow.

**A group of five people in a mid-sized city who want to do something about the state of the world but don't know where to start**
They form a team around a specific territory — a neighborhood, a local watershed, a specific school. They take on one real experiment together: something modest, something visible, something that can be honestly evaluated. They document what they learn. Over time they take on harder experiments. Two years in, they've built something that didn't exist — a genuine local culture of people doing serious work together. Other people in the city find them through the map and want to join or form adjacent teams.

**A therapist or facilitator who sees clients making progress in sessions but not in their lives**
They discover the experiment library and the team structure. They realize that what their clients need is not more processing — it's a context, a team, a real set of practices they can bring into their lives between sessions. They become a doorway for their clients into the game. Over time they become one of the game's most powerful nodes — not because of their credentials, but because of what they've built and what they've gone through.

**A community already doing regenerative work — land, food, education — but feeling isolated and burned out**
They're already building next culture, but they're doing it alone and they don't have the maps to understand what they're doing or why it's hard. They find the game and recognize it immediately as the framework for what they've been attempting. They connect to the network. They discover that what felt like their idiosyncratic struggle is actually a common pattern that others have navigated. They gain tools, allies, and the sense of being part of a larger movement. The isolation lifts. The burnout has somewhere to go.

**A young person who knows the world they were promised isn't coming**
They grew up knowing that the institutions are failing, the climate is destabilizing, the social fabric is fraying. They're not cynical — they're sharp. They want to do something real, but everything on offer looks like either performance or compromise. They find the game and discover that the framework takes their intelligence seriously. The experiments are hard. The team is demanding. The work is real. For the first time they feel like they're doing something that matches the scale of what's actually happening.

## What This Is Not

It is not a workshop you attend and return from. The game is your life — or at least, the part of your life that you're taking seriously.

It is not a community of like-minded people. The team you work with will challenge you. The experiments will expose you. The map will show you how far there is to go.

It is not spiritual bypassing dressed in new language. The game is more interested in what you actually do than in what you believe.

It is not for everyone, and that's fine. It's for people at the edge. There are more of them than you think, and they're ready.
